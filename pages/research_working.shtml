Physics Education Research

Marshall's area of specialization is in physics education research (PER). PER is an example of discipline-based education research, in that it is generally conducted by researchers in a physics department, most often with formal academic training in physics. Physics education researchers are uniquely well-suited to study the teaching and learning of physics because they are intimately well-acquainted with the subject matter, and they are scientists first and foremost.

As the name suggests, physics education research is primarily concerned with improving the teaching and learning of physics. Learning is very complicated, so there are many sub-fields and specializations under the blanket of PER. As with all science, theoretical modeling and experimental data collection are involved and are rarely independent. Most groups have a primary thematic focus, like one or more of these examples:

  • K-12 teacher training and scientific literacy
  • Progression of development from novice-like to expert-like reasoning
  • Instructional practice and classroom structure
  • Student attitudes toward physics or science
    • <\ul>

    The primary focus of the Physics Education Group at the University of Washington (UWPEG) is understanding and addressing student conceptual difficulties in physics. Student understanding is probed and researched via carefully constructed pretests and post-tests; detailed curricular materials are created to help students recognize, assess, and build on their own model; researchers are also instructors in the class and can observe first-hand how students respond to the curricula. The process of development of curriculum, research, and instruction is rarely linear, and most often there is a complex inter-play between these three facets.

    UWPEG has released two primary curricula based on and validated by research, Physics by Inquiry and Tutorials in Introductory Physics. While both are still in active development, these resources are widely used in the US, with several international sites using them as well. For more information, visit the UWPEG web site.
