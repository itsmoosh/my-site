Physics Education Research

Marshall's area of specialization is in physics education research (PER). PER is an example of discipline-based education research, in that it is generally conducted by researchers in a physics department, most often with formal academic training in physics. Physics education researchers are uniquely well-suited to study the teaching and learning of physics because they are intimately well-acquainted with the subject matter, and they are scientists first and foremost.

As the name suggests, physics education research is primarily concerned with improving the teaching and learning of physics. Learning is very complicated, so there are many sub-fields and specializations under the blanket of PER. As with all science, theoretical modeling and experimental data collection are involved and are rarely independent. Most groups have a primary thematic focus, like one or more of these examples:

  • K-12 teacher training and scientific literacy
  • Progression of development from novice-like to expert-like reasoning
  • Instructional practice and classroom structure
  • Student attitudes toward physics or science

The primary focus of the Physics Education Group at the University of Washington (UWPEG) is understanding and addressing student conceptual difficulties in physics. Student understanding is probed and researched via carefully constructed pretests and post-tests; detailed curricular materials are created to help students recognize, assess, and build on their own model; researchers are also instructors in the class and can observe first-hand how students respond to the curricula. The process of development of curriculum, research, and instruction is rarely linear, and most often there is a complex inter-play between these three facets.

UWPEG has released two primary curricula based on and validated by research, Physics by Inquiry and Tutorials in Introductory Physics. While both are still in active development, these resources are widely used in the US, with several international sites using them as well. For more information, visit the UWPEG web site.

Research Focus: Doing more with less

Budgeting time is challenging. As an instructor, choices about what to cover and in how much detail are always close at hand. Much of Marshall's research is devoted to maximizing what students take away from class-related activities, especially in-class group work and homework. Getting more out of the time spent, or "doing more with less," is the main goal. Occupying less time allows for other fruitful in-class activities, and everyone wants students to learn more from the time they spend.

A strong research tradition precedes Marshall, and he carries it forward with pride. The Tutorials in Introductory Physics created by current and former members of UWPEG have seen decades of research, validation, instruction, and gradual improvement. The research literature is always growing, and there are often golden nuggets to be found therein that can help enhance the Tutorials. Marshall's recent work has centered on poring over tutorials from Electricity and Magnetism, aiming to condense topics to take less in-class time to cover.

The Tutorials are not only based on research on difficult topics, they are also carefully validated. Careful studies controlling for students' experiences demonstrate that each tutorial worksheet has an overall impact, helping many students learn the concepts better than alternatives like solving textbook problems. To maintain this research validation, every major change (like combining tutorials) is followed by another careful validation study—we want students to learn as well or better than before. Marshall is in the midst of a study re-validating several tutorials he reconfigured to take less time.

Supporting his work on improving time spent in-class, Marshall is beginning a project for characterizing the types of student behavior when working through tutorials in groups. Understanding how students engage with the questions and their group can be used to guide the development of better tutorial worksheets—by ensuring students are maximizing effective behaviors like explaining their reasoning to each other, we ensure they get the most out of their time with their group and instructors.

On the horizon, Marshall will also be studying avenues to combine interactive simulations with student homework assignments, with the ultimate goal of improving what students learn from completing homework without spending more time than they do already. Many research studies show great potential for student learning with interactive simulations, like the PhETs. Students are always under a burdensome, stressful workload, however. We can't simply ask them to spend their free time using a simulation, and cutting out other effective assignments might do more harm than good. That's where Marshall's careful research and experience rewriting curriculum comes in. He'll be consulting the research literature to form a well-grounded plan, choosing which exercises to remove or tailor to an added simulation in order for students to get the most out of the time they spend on homework.
