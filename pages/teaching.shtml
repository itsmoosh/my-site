Teaching overview

Learning is complicated. Everyone responds differently to the same stimuli, including their interactions with an instructor. A positive learning experience isn't simply related to whether or not a course is taught by a "good" instructor. Nevertheless, an experienced, responsive instructor in a conducive classroom setting can facilitate student learning.

Well-designed instructional materials can also lend a lot of support to the goal of students learning as much course content as possible. The Tutorials in Introductory Physics, researched, developed, and validated all by the Physics Education Group at UW, compose just such a supporting material. All calculus-based introductory physics courses at UW involve a supplementary tutorial section, were students work in small groups to discuss a topic covered in the tutorial workbook.

In tutorial sections, two teaching assistants (TAs) act as navigators, guiding up to six groups of 2-4 students with reflective questioning. Each week, the TAs meet to work through the tutorials with an experienced TA so that they are better prepared to address student questions and difficulties during in-class sessions. Marshall has been a tutorial TA for eight quarters, most often in the Electricity & Magnetism introductory course. He also leads groups of TAs during weekly preparation meetings.

Teaching philosophy

Several studies have convincingly concluded that compared to traditional instructional strategies, learning is best enhanced when students are actively engaged--analyzing, interpreting, explaining, or otherwise exploring the subject matter themselves. In this context, "traditional" implies didactic instruction, centered around reading reference textbooks written by experts and frequent lecture presentations by the instructor, also an expert. A large and growing body of research in physics education has shown that experts describing their knowledge and making unsubstantiated assertions to students is not an effective way to encourage learning.

Marshall takes his teaching seriously. The primary guiding principle behind his classroom demeanor is always one of the first announcements he makes in class:

I am here to help you learn.

Students are encouraged to ask questions by email, day or night. Office hours are also available upon request or during scheduled study center hours (see the table above for scheduling).

Marshall aims to keep students engaged and he encourages every student to participate in group discussions. Engagement is sustained through periodic discussions with students, frequently involving both open-ended and guiding questions. These questions are intended to not only to help students stay on track, but also to give students immediate feedback on their explanations. Tutorial assessments (homework and free-response exams) always require a thorough explanation of reasoning alongside their response to a qualitative question, one where the response does not require a numerical calculation. The immediate feedback an attentive instructor can provide to students during class time can be very valuable to both student and instructor, especially when students are asked to explain their reasoning in detail.

Student testimonials

At the conclusion of each term at UW, the Office of Educational Assessment administers course evaluations. Students' opinions about their experiences are solicited in a variety of ways, including free-response questions. Marshall reviews all of his course evaluations, both to get a sense of how he is doing managing the class and to find suggestions for improvement.
